Items land here when AI confidence is below 60%. Rather than silently mis-classifying, the app admits uncertainty and invites the user to sort. The framing is "3 saves need a home" â€” helpful, not apologetic. The user is a curator, not a janitor fixing AI mistakes.

The Confidence Spectrum

Three tiers of classification confidence, three visual treatments:

High (80%+): Normal card. No indicators. This is the default â€” the AI is confident, the card looks clean. 70-80% of saves.

Medium (60-80%): Dashed border + "Maybe?" badge + "Move?" link in footer. The card lives in the AI's best-guess stack but quietly invites correction. 10-20% of saves.

Low (<60%): Goes to Inbox for triage (Screen 9). Never placed in a stack with false confidence. 5-15% of saves.

The "Maybe?" Badge

Visible but not alarming. The dashed border is the primary signal â€” subtle enough that users who don't care can ignore it. The "Move?" link in the source line is the action affordance. Users who notice it can tap to reassign with one touch. Users who don't notice it keep scrolling â€” no harm done.

Inline Reassignment

Tapping "Move?" expands an inline card (not a full modal or new screen) showing the item preview and AI's alternative suggestions with confidence percentages. One tap to move, or "Keep Here" to confirm the current stack and boost its confidence. The grid behind dims to 40% to focus attention.

Context-Aware Ambiguity

The weekender bag example is perfect: it's Travel (travel gear) or Shopping (product to buy). The AI can't know the user's intent. The "Maybe?" treatment lets the user resolve this naturally without the AI pretending to be certain. These ambiguous items are the most valuable training signals â€” they teach the AI about the user's mental model.

Design Principles

1. "Your phone already knows." The entire brand message is: your expensive phone has an AI chip in it. We put it to work. You save things. It organizes them. Privately. Instantly. No cloud. No waiting. The phone you already own is the smartest filing system you've ever had.
2. Zero-configuration intelligence. The app should never ask the user to "set up categories" or "configure your preferences" or "choose your interests." Everything is inferred from what they save. The AI-generated filter chips in the stack view (Bali, Japan, Italy) appear organically as the user saves content. The app configures itself.
3. Speed is the feature. Share extension: under 2 seconds. Search results: instant (offline). Opening a stack: instant (local data). Classification: under 3 seconds. If any interaction takes more than 3 seconds, users will stop saving. Speed must be treated as a core feature, not a performance metric.
4. Show the AI's work, subtly. Don't hide the intelligence. When AI extracts a location, show "ğŸ“ Santorini, Greece" on the card. When it generates a summary, show it prominently. When filter chips appear automatically, the user notices "I didn't create this â€” the app figured it out." This builds trust in the AI and justifies the value proposition. But never be loud about it â€” no "AI-powered!" badges. Let the intelligence speak through the UI.
5. Be honest about timing. Never show data you don't have. The share extension shows a domain name and stack icon â€” not a fake title that hasn't loaded yet. Cards start minimal and fill in as enrichment completes. Shimmer placeholders signal "more is coming" without pretending it's already here. The user trusts the app because it never lies about its state. A save gets smarter over time â€” and the UI makes that visible without making it alarming.
